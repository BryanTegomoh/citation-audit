## 3. Article Content: Four High-Value Care Advice Statements

Harris et al. 2016 contains four High-Value Care Advice Statements for acute respiratory tract infections in adults:

| Statement | Condition | Recommendation |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | Acute bronchitis | Clinicians should NOT test or treat with antibiotics (unless pneumonia suspected) |
| 2 | Pharyngitis (sore throat) | Test ONLY when clinical features suggest GAS (Centor criteria). Treat with antibiotics ONLY if test-confirmed |
| 3 | Acute rhinosinusitis | Symptomatic management. Reserve antibiotics for persistent symptoms (>10 days), high fever (>=39C) with purulent discharge >=3 days, or worsening after improvement ("double-sickening") |
| 4 | Common cold | Clinicians should NOT prescribe antibiotics |

## 4. Claim-by-Claim Verification Against OpenEvidence

### Claim 1: ABRS diagnostic criteria [4-5]

**OpenEvidence claim:**
> "Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis: Persistent symptoms >10 days, high fever with purulent discharge for >=3 consecutive days, or worsening after initial improvement ('double-sickening')"

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.**

Harris 2016, Statement 3 discusses all three ABRS diagnostic criteria. These criteria originate from the IDSA clinical practice guidelines (Chow et al. 2012, doi:10.1093/cid/cir1043), which Harris et al. cite. The ACP/CDC advice paper adopted and disseminated these criteria for primary care audiences.

Citation [5] (Libman 2017) is paired here as [4-5] and corroborates by applying the same criteria in a clinical Grand Rounds context.

---

### Claim 2: 85% decrease in antibiotic prescribing attributed to "chest cold" labeling [4]

**OpenEvidence claim (paraphrased):**
> Labeling bronchitis as "chest cold" achieves an 85% decrease in antibiotic prescribing

**Verdict: INTERVENTION MISATTRIBUTED.**

**The 85% figure is real.** It comes from:

> Mangione-Smith R, Zhou C, Robinson JD, Taylor JA, Elliott MN, Heritage J. Communication Practices and Antibiotic Use for Acute Respiratory Tract Infections in Children. *Annals of Family Medicine*. 2015;13(3):221-227. doi:10.1370/afm.1785. PMID: 25964399.

Mangione-Smith et al. found an adjusted risk ratio (aRR) of 0.15 (85% reduction) when providers combined **positive treatment recommendations** (symptomatic therapy advice) with **negative treatment recommendations** (explanation of why antibiotics were not needed). This is a combined communication strategy, not a diagnostic relabeling intervention.

**Harris 2016 correctly cites Mangione-Smith 2015 as reference (64).** The paper's text discusses the importance of combining positive and negative treatment recommendations as a communication strategy that reduces prescribing. Harris does NOT attribute this 85% to "chest cold" labeling.

**The "chest cold" labeling intervention comes from a different study:**

> Phillips TG, Hickner J. Calling Acute Bronchitis a Chest Cold May Improve Patient Satisfaction With Appropriate Antibiotic Use. *Journal of the American Board of Family Medicine*. 2005;18(6):459-463. PMID: 16322409.

Phillips & Hickner 2005 measured **patient satisfaction** with the diagnosis, not prescribing rates. That study found patients were more satisfied with the term "chest cold" compared to "bronchitis" when told antibiotics were unnecessary. It did not find or claim an 85% reduction in prescribing.

**The error:** OpenEvidence conflates two distinct interventions:
1. Combined communication strategies (Mangione-Smith 2015): 85% prescribing reduction (aRR 0.15)
2. "Chest cold" diagnostic labeling (Phillips & Hickner 2005): patient satisfaction improvement

Then attributes the combined result (85%) to the wrong intervention ("chest cold" labeling), citing Harris 2016, which correctly references the Mangione-Smith finding but does not misattribute it.

**Clinical consequence:** A provider implementing a "chest cold" labeling strategy alone and expecting an 85% prescribing reduction would be acting on a misattributed finding. The 85% comes from a combined communication intervention that includes both symptomatic therapy recommendations AND explanations of antibiotic inappropriateness.

### Claim 3: Peer comparison as a stewardship intervention [4] and [7]

**OpenEvidence claim:**
> "Audit and feedback mechanisms, including provider prescribing dashboards with peer comparison" [4] and [7]

**Verdict: MISATTRIBUTED to [4].**

Harris et al. 2016 does NOT describe a peer comparison intervention. The paper is a guideline/advice document with four clinical recommendations. It does not implement, test, or describe peer comparison as a stewardship strategy.

**The actual source for peer comparison as a stewardship intervention is:**

> Meeker D, Linder JA, Fox CR, et al. Effect of Behavioral Interventions on Inappropriate Antibiotic Prescribing Among Primary Care Practices: A Randomized Clinical Trial. *JAMA*. 2016;315(6):562-570. doi:10.1001/jama.2016.0275. PMID: 26864410.

Meeker et al. (2016) tested three behavioral interventions:
1. Suggested alternatives (decision support)
2. Accountable justification (requiring documented rationale)
3. **Peer comparison** (periodic email feedback comparing each clinician's prescribing rate to top performers)

The peer comparison arm showed significant reductions in inappropriate prescribing.

Harris 2016 may reference Meeker or similar studies in its background, but it is not the source of the peer comparison intervention evidence. Attributing "peer comparison" findings to Harris 2016 is misattribution.

### Claim 4: Delayed prescribing for rhinosinusitis [4]

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.**

Harris 2016, Statement 3 discusses delayed prescribing as an option for acute rhinosinusitis, consistent with the paper's approach to conditional antibiotic use. The paper discusses providing prescriptions that patients fill only if symptoms worsen or fail to improve.

---

### Claim 5: Patient satisfaction depends on communication, not antibiotics [4]

**Verdict: PLAUSIBLE but likely derived from other studies.**

Harris 2016 discusses communication strategies and references research on patient satisfaction. However, the specific finding that satisfaction depends on communication quality rather than antibiotic receipt comes from studies like Mangione-Smith et al. and others in the communication literature. Harris 2016 may reference these findings, but it does not generate this evidence.

## 5. Citation Overloading Pattern

Citation [4] is used by OpenEvidence to support claims originating from at least four distinct research programs:

| Claim | Actual Source | Harris 2016 Relationship |
|-------|-------------|--------------------------|
| ABRS criteria | Chow et al. 2012 (IDSA) | Adopted and cites as reference |
| 85% prescribing reduction | Mangione-Smith et al. 2015 | Correctly cites as reference (64) |
| "Chest cold" labeling | Phillips & Hickner 2005 | Not the source of this intervention |
| Peer comparison | Meeker et al. 2016 | Not described in this paper |
| Patient satisfaction = communication | Multiple studies | Referenced but not generated |

This is citation overloading: a single paper is made to serve as the citation for findings from multiple independent research teams. The guideline paper legitimately references some of these studies, but using it as the citation for their specific findings obscures the actual evidence sources.
