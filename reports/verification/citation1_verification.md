## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: CDC high-priority targets

- **Claim:** "CDC identifies nonspecific upper respiratory infections, viral pharyngitis, uncomplicated acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, and acute otitis media as high-priority targets"
- **Verdict:** CONFIRMED (with minor paraphrase)
- **Evidence:** The article states: "The CDC's review of antibiotic stewardship identified high-priority conditions to target for improving antibiotic prescribing, including nonspecific URI, viral pharyngitis, uncomplicated acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, and AOM."
- **Notes:** OpenEvidence uses the full terms where the article uses abbreviations (URI, AOM). The claim is substantively accurate. The article says "high-priority conditions to target for improving antibiotic prescribing" rather than "high-priority targets" alone, which is a reasonable paraphrase.
- **Source:** AAFP full text article page

### Claim 2: Group A streptococcal pharyngitis treatment

- **Claim:** "Group A streptococcal pharyngitis: Confirmed by rapid antigen detection test or culture; treatment with amoxicillin or penicillin V reduces symptom duration and prevents acute rheumatic fever"
- **Verdict:** CONFIRMED (with paraphrase; accurate in substance but not verbatim)
- **Evidence:** The article states two relevant passages:
  1. On diagnosis: The IDSA recommendation for throat swab rapid antigen detection test (RADT) is discussed. The article mentions that backup throat cultures should be done in children with negative RADT results.
  2. On treatment: "A 10-day course of penicillin V or amoxicillin is the first-line therapy for group A beta-hemolytic streptococcal pharyngitis."
  3. On purpose of treatment: "Treatment of group A beta-hemolytic streptococcal pharyngitis with appropriate antibiotics helps prevent the rare complications of acute rheumatic fever and peritonsillar abscess, hastens clinical resolution, and decreases contagion."
- **Notes:** The OpenEvidence claim compresses these elements accurately. However, the article says treatment "hastens clinical resolution" (not "reduces symptom duration") and "helps prevent the rare complications of acute rheumatic fever" (qualified with "rare," which OpenEvidence omits). The omission of "rare" subtly overstates the benefit framing but does not misrepresent the claim.
- **Source:** AAFP full text article page

### Claim 3: Delayed prescribing strategies

- **Claim:** "Delayed prescribing strategies offer antibiotics to be filled only if symptoms worsen or fail to improve within several days"
- **Verdict:** CONFIRMED (paraphrased from two distinct passages)
- **Evidence:** The article contains two relevant statements:
  1. General: "Delayed prescribing reduced antibiotic use but also decreased patient satisfaction and increased symptom duration."
  2. For AOM specifically: "Through joint decision-making, parents can be provided a prescription during the initial visit to be filled if the child does not improve or worsens within two to three days."
- **Notes:** The OpenEvidence claim merges the general concept of delayed prescribing with the specific AOM language about filling prescriptions if symptoms worsen. The article's general statement about delayed prescribing does not use the "offer antibiotics to be filled only if" phrasing; that language comes from the AOM-specific section. The claim is substantively accurate but conflates two passages and generalizes a condition-specific recommendation. The article also notes that delayed prescribing "decreased patient satisfaction and increased symptom duration," which OpenEvidence omits entirely, presenting the strategy without its documented drawbacks.
- **Source:** AAFP full text article page

### Claim 4: Combining positive and negative treatment recommendations

- **Claim:** "Combining positive treatment recommendations with negative recommendations decreases prescribing and improves satisfaction"
- **Verdict:** CONFIRMED
- **Evidence:** The article states: "A combination of positive treatment recommendations (i.e., suggestions to reduce a patient's symptoms) with negative treatment recommendations (i.e., explanations of the inappropriateness of antibiotics) was associated with decreased antibiotic prescribing and higher visit satisfaction."
- **Notes:** Accurate paraphrase. The article says "higher visit satisfaction" where OpenEvidence says "improves satisfaction." Substantively equivalent.
- **Source:** AAFP full text article page

### Claim 5: Less than 1% penicillin allergy confirmation

- **Claim:** "less than 1% of patients reporting penicillin allergy have confirmed allergy"
- **Verdict:** CONFIRMED (with minor reframing)
- **Evidence:** The article states: "Approximately 10% of people in the United States report having a penicillin allergy; however, less than 1% of the population has a confirmed penicillin allergy."
- **Notes:** The article says "less than 1% of the population" has a confirmed allergy; OpenEvidence says "less than 1% of patients reporting penicillin allergy have confirmed allergy." These are different denominators. The article's "less than 1% of the population" means roughly 3.3 million people. "Less than 1% of those reporting allergy" would mean less than 1% of the 10% who report it, or less than 0.1% of the population. This is a meaningful mathematical misrepresentation of the statistic. The article compares 10% (self-reported) vs. less than 1% (confirmed) of the general population, not less than 1% of the self-reporters.

  However, this is a known clinical fact that other sources (e.g., Shenoy et al., JAMA 2019) do frame as "less than 1% of those labeled as penicillin allergic truly have IgE-mediated allergy" when using skin testing. The Sur/Plesa article uses the population-denominator framing, not the reporter-denominator framing. OpenEvidence may have conflated the two framings.
- **Source:** AAFP full text article page

### Claim 8: Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis duration

- **Claim:** "Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis duration of 5-7 days (adults) and 10-14 days (children)"
- **Verdict:** CONFIRMED (but this refers to treatment duration, not illness duration)
- **Evidence:** The article states: "amoxicillin/clavulanate (Augmentin) is the first-line treatment in patients with acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, with a treatment duration of five to seven days in adults and 10 to 14 days in children."
- **Notes:** The 5-7 days and 10-14 days refer to antibiotic treatment duration, not the duration of the illness itself. OpenEvidence's phrasing "duration of 5-7 days" is ambiguous and could be misread as illness duration. The article is clear this is treatment duration for amoxicillin/clavulanate.
- **Source:** AAFP full text article page

## Overall Assessment

**Citation validity:** The paper exists and is correctly cited (authors, journal, year, volume, issue, pages).

**Claim accuracy:** 8 of 8 primary claims are traceable to content in this paper. However, several involve meaningful paraphrasing that introduces subtle distortions:

1. **Denominator shift (Claim 5):** The most significant error. Changing "less than 1% of the population" to "less than 1% of patients reporting allergy" changes the statistic by an order of magnitude. Both statements convey that penicillin allergy is over-reported, but the math is different.

2. **Omission of drawbacks (Claim 3):** The article explicitly states delayed prescribing "decreased patient satisfaction and increased symptom duration." Presenting the strategy without its trade-offs misrepresents the article's balanced assessment.

3. **Qualifier omission (Claim 2):** The article calls rheumatic fever prevention a benefit for "the rare complications." Dropping "rare" subtly overstates the clinical significance.

4. **Ambiguity (Claim 8):** The 5-7 day and 10-14 day figures are treatment durations, not illness durations. The OpenEvidence phrasing does not clearly specify this.

**No fabricated claims detected.** All claims map to real content in the paper. The errors are distortions of framing, not hallucinations.

**The "85% decrease" and "chest cold" language are not in this paper.** If OpenEvidence attributes these to Citation [1], that would constitute misattribution.
