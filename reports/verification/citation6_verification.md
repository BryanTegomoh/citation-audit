## Claim Under Verification

**OpenEvidence attributes this claim to [6]:**

> "Community-acquired pneumonia: Amoxicillin, doxycycline, or macrolides (in areas with <25% pneumococcal resistance) for otherwise healthy adults"

### Question 1: Does the paper discuss all three options (amoxicillin, doxycycline, macrolides)?

- **Verdict:** VERY LIKELY YES
- **Evidence:** The article is a Clinical Practice review covering outpatient CAP treatment. Multiple secondary summaries of this article and of the ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines (which File & Ramirez review) confirm that the standard outpatient recommendations for healthy adults without comorbidities include: amoxicillin 1g TID, doxycycline 100mg BID, or a macrolide (azithromycin or clarithromycin) with the macrolide resistance caveat. File & Ramirez, as the authors of this Clinical Practice review, would be expected to present these ATS/IDSA recommendations, as that is the purpose of this article type.
- **Limitation:** The NEJM full text is paywalled. Verification is based on: (a) the article's stated scope ("choice of antimicrobial therapy varies according to severity, coexisting conditions, and the likelihood of antimicrobial-resistant organisms"), (b) consistent reporting across secondary sources summarizing the article, (c) the known content of the ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines that this Clinical Practice article reviews, and (d) the PPTX summary slides available from NEJM.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. A Clinical Practice review on CAP by two leading infectious disease experts (File is a co-author of the ATS/IDSA guidelines themselves) that does not discuss amoxicillin, doxycycline, and macrolides for outpatient treatment would be professionally negligent. The question is not whether these drugs are discussed but how exactly the macrolide caveat is worded.

### Question 2: Does the paper specify the "<25% pneumococcal macrolide resistance" threshold?

- **Verdict:** LIKELY YES, but this threshold originates from the ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines, not from File & Ramirez 2023 as independent research.
- **Evidence chain:**
  - The ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines (Metlay et al., *Am J Respir Crit Care Med*. 2019;200(7):e45-e67; PMC6812437) explicitly state: macrolide monotherapy is recommended "only in areas with pneumococcal resistance to macrolides <25%" with a "conditional recommendation, moderate quality of evidence."
  - The ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines note that this represents "a departure from the prior CAP guidelines" and that the panel "did not give a strong recommendation for routine use of a macrolide antibiotic as monotherapy for outpatient CAP, even in patients without comorbidities."
  - The rationale: "studies of macrolide failures in patients with macrolide-resistant S. pneumoniae, in combination with a macrolide resistance rate of >30% among S. pneumoniae isolates in the United States."
  - Thomas M. File, Jr. was a member of the ATS/IDSA guideline committee. As a Clinical Practice review, this NEJM article would present these guideline recommendations, including the 25% threshold.
  - Multiple secondary sources (AAFP summary, PcMED Project, PMC11212456, Medscape guidelines summary) all attribute the <25% threshold to the ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines, not to File & Ramirez 2023 independently.
- **Critical distinction:** The <25% threshold is a **guideline recommendation** from ATS/IDSA 2019, which File & Ramirez 2023 almost certainly presents and discusses as a Clinical Practice review. File & Ramirez did not independently derive this threshold; they reviewed and summarized the existing guideline.

### Question 3: Is the "<25% resistance" qualifier in the OpenEvidence response appropriate?

- **Verdict:** YES, the qualifier is clinically appropriate and reflects genuine guideline nuance.
- **Analysis:** The OpenEvidence response states: "macrolides (in areas with <25% pneumococcal resistance)." This is:
  - Consistent with the ATS/IDSA 2019 conditional recommendation
  - Clinically important because US pneumococcal macrolide resistance averages approximately 30% nationally, meaning macrolide monotherapy is inappropriate in most US settings
  - A meaningful qualification that demonstrates clinical awareness, not an inappropriate addition
- **The attribution question:** OpenEvidence attributes this to File & Ramirez 2023 [6]. File & Ramirez 2023, as a Clinical Practice review, would be expected to present the ATS/IDSA 2019 guideline recommendation including this threshold. Citing the review article (which summarizes the guideline) rather than the primary guideline document itself is acceptable practice, though citing Metlay et al. 2019 (the actual ATS/IDSA guideline) would be more precise.
